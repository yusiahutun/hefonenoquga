Alabama's hospital for insane housed patients in 'concentration camp' conditions

Publish date: 2024-05-29

Dirt and rubble lie strewn on the ground, while a staircase flanked by mouldy walls leads to nowhere.

It may seem dilapidated and eerie now but life was very different here in 1861 - when the Alabama State Hospital for the Insane first opened its doors.

The facility, in Tuscaloosa, was originally built to house 250 patients with mental health issues.

Surrounded by acres of farmland, the idea was to give those living there dignity - residents could become part of a community, tending the fields, working at the dairy, or perhaps a job in the kitchen. 

Thomas Kirkbride, an activist in the 1830s, was a major force behind the state hospital project which began in earnest in 1853. Together with fellow moral campaigner Dorothea Dix, they had a vision of a beautifully ornate building.

And that vision became a reality by 1859: Italianate architecture with three wings; a billiards room; tennis court; library; and chapel. All this with gas lighting and state-of-the-art central heating too.

As time passed, further wings were added. But along with the expansion came eventual decline.

In the following decades, funding was cut and the number of patients increased to the point of bursting, totalling around 5,200 - and not enough staff to look after them.

In 1970, Alabama was the lowest-ranking U.S. state for mental health funding. Hundreds of employees were laid off in the same year.

Conditions at the time were considered so squalid at Bryce Hospital that the editor of the Montgomery Advertiser likened it to a concentration camp, according to the Crimson White.

Given new government mandates after a lawsuit against the hospital's 'intolerable conditions', demolition began. First to go were the outermost wings and farm buildings.

By the turn of the century, only the original building and its six wings were standing; but it continued to provide support, albeit for a smaller number of patients. 

Salvation arrived in 2010, when the University of Alabama, whose sprawling campus surrounded the facility, bought the historic hospital.

In dire need of renovation - and still housing 250 patients - the complex was too big for the state Department of Mental Health to manage. 

In 2014, the university started a $40million restoration scheme, having moved the remaining patients to a new facility nearby, according to AL.com.

This forms part of a $121million project that includes a new performing arts center on the property, and is scheduled for completion in 2020.
